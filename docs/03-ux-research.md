# UX Research

## Overview

This project currently uses proto-research and assumption-based UX analysis.

Since NoteFlow is a portfolio case study, full user research has not been completed yet. However, the project is based on logical assumptions about beginner music students and their possible challenges with note reading.

In a future version, I would validate these assumptions through interviews and usability testing with beginner music students.

---

## Research Goal

The goal of the research is to understand the main challenges beginner music students face when practicing note reading.

The research focuses on these questions:

* What makes note reading difficult for beginners?
* What type of practice feels helpful and motivating?
* How should feedback be shown after a correct or wrong answer?
* Does progress tracking help users stay motivated?
* What should a beginner-friendly music practice app feel like?

---

## Target User

The target user is a beginner music student.

This user may be:

* Learning piano or basic music theory
* Practicing note reading for the first time
* Struggling to recognize notes quickly
* Looking for short and simple practice
* Needing encouragement and clear feedback

---

## Assumptions

Based on the project idea, I assumed that beginner music students may:

* Recognize notes slowly
* Confuse some notes on the staff
* Feel bored by long exercises
* Feel stressed when they make mistakes
* Prefer short daily practice sessions
* Need instant feedback after answering
* Feel motivated when they can see progress

---

## Research Questions

If this project is developed further, I would ask beginner music students these questions:

1. Which part of note reading is most difficult for you?
2. Do you usually practice note reading on paper, with a teacher, or with an app?
3. Do you prefer short practice sessions or longer exercises?
4. What kind of feedback helps you after a wrong answer?
5. Does seeing your progress make you more motivated?
6. Which notes do you confuse most often?
7. What would make a note-reading app feel less stressful?
8. Would you use a daily 5-minute practice feature?

---

## Possible Interview Participants

For future research, I would interview:

* Beginner piano students
* Students learning music theory
* People who recently started learning an instrument
* Music learners who use mobile apps for practice

A small first round of research could include 2–3 beginner users.

---

## Key Insights

Based on the initial assumptions, the main insights are:

### 1. Beginners need simple practice

The app should avoid complex instructions and focus on one clear task at a time.

### 2. Practice should feel short and achievable

A short daily practice session can feel easier and less stressful for beginners.

### 3. Feedback should be immediate

Users should quickly understand whether their answer is correct or wrong.

### 4. Mistakes should feel useful

Wrong answers should help users understand what they need to review, instead of making them feel discouraged.

### 5. Progress should be visible

Progress tracking can help users stay motivated and continue practicing.

---

## Design Decisions Based on Research

These insights influenced the main design decisions of NoteFlow:

* The app uses short practice sessions.
* The Practice Screen focuses on one question at a time.
* The Result Screen shows score and accuracy clearly.
* Weak notes are shown so users know what to review.
* The Progress Screen helps users see improvement over time.
* The visual style is calm, soft, and beginner-friendly.

---

## Limitations

This research is not based on full real-user interviews yet.

It is an early-stage UX research plan and proto-research document created for a UX/UI portfolio project.

The next step would be to test the prototype with beginner music students and update the design based on real feedback.

---

## Summary

The UX research direction for NoteFlow focuses on understanding how beginner music students practice note reading, what makes the experience difficult, and how a mobile app can make practice shorter, clearer, and more motivating.

The current version is based on assumptions, and future user interviews would help validate and improve the design.
