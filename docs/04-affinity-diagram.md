# Affinity Diagram

## Overview

An affinity diagram is used to organize research notes into meaningful groups.

For NoteFlow, this affinity diagram is based on proto-research and assumption-based UX analysis. Since full user interviews have not been completed yet, the notes below represent possible user needs and pain points for beginner music students.

---

## Research Notes

Possible user statements:

* I recognize notes slowly.
* I sometimes confuse notes on the staff.
* Some notes look very similar to me.
* Long exercises feel boring.
* I prefer short daily practice.
* I want to know immediately if my answer is correct.
* I feel discouraged when I make mistakes.
* I want feedback that helps me improve.
* Seeing my progress would motivate me.
* I want to know which notes I should review.
* A simple app would make practice easier.
* I do not want the app to feel like a stressful exam.

---

## Group 1: Note Recognition Problems

### Notes

* I recognize notes slowly.
* I sometimes confuse notes on the staff.
* Some notes look very similar to me.

### Insight

Beginner music students may need focused practice to improve note recognition speed and accuracy.

### Design Decision

NoteFlow includes a Practice Screen with one note question at a time and multiple-choice answers to keep the task simple and focused.

---

## Group 2: Need for Short Practice

### Notes

* Long exercises feel boring.
* I prefer short daily practice.
* A simple app would make practice easier.

### Insight

Beginners may be more likely to practice if the session feels short, simple, and achievable.

### Design Decision

NoteFlow uses a short practice model, such as a 5-minute note-reading exercise.

---

## Group 3: Need for Clear Feedback

### Notes

* I want to know immediately if my answer is correct.
* I feel discouraged when I make mistakes.
* I want feedback that helps me improve.

### Insight

Feedback should be immediate, clear, and supportive. Mistakes should help the user learn instead of making the experience stressful.

### Design Decision

NoteFlow includes answer states such as Default, Selected, Correct, and Wrong. The Result Screen also shows accuracy and weak notes.

---

## Group 4: Motivation and Progress

### Notes

* Seeing my progress would motivate me.
* I want to know which notes I should review.
* I do not want the app to feel like a stressful exam.

### Insight

Progress tracking can help users stay motivated, but it should feel encouraging rather than stressful.

### Design Decision

NoteFlow includes a Progress Screen with practice streak, average accuracy, weekly chart, weak notes, and recommended practice.

---

## Final Insights

The affinity diagram helped organize the main user needs into four key areas:

1. Note recognition problems
2. Short and simple practice
3. Clear and supportive feedback
4. Motivation through progress tracking

---

## Impact on Product Design

Based on these groups, NoteFlow focuses on:

* Short note-reading exercises
* Simple multiple-choice questions
* Instant feedback
* Weak note review
* Progress tracking
* Calm and beginner-friendly UI

---

## Summary

The affinity diagram shows that beginner music students need more than just practice questions. They need a simple, supportive, and motivating learning experience.

This helped shape NoteFlow as a mobile app focused on short practice, clear feedback, and visible progress.
